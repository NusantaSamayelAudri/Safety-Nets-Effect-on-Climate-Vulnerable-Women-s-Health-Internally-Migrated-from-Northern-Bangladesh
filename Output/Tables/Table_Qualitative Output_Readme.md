## Mixed-Methods Integration Note

The table below summarizes how qualitative evidence from interviews, FGDs, and KIIs helps interpret the quantitative findings. While the statistical analysis identifies associations, the qualitative data explain the mechanisms behind those patterns.

## Qualitative Findings Summary

The qualitative component explains why safety-net access does not automatically translate into improved health outcomes. Interviews, focus group discussions, and key informant interviews highlighted institutional, economic, and infrastructural barriers shaping women’s vulnerability.

| Qualitative Theme | Evidence from Fieldwork | Link to Quantitative Findings | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Administrative exclusion | Women reported difficulty accessing support due to missing documents, lack of permanent address, and weak institutional recognition | Helps explain why safety-net receipt does not independently predict good health | Formal eligibility does not guarantee usable access to protection |
| Local gatekeeping and informal mediation | Access to aid often depended on local leaders, community intermediaries, NGO contacts, or political connections | Supports the finding that safety-net effects are uneven across respondents | Safety-net access is shaped by social and institutional power, not need alone |
| Hidden costs of healthcare | Respondents described informal fees, medicine costs, and transport costs as barriers to treatment | Reinforces the strong role of clinic access in predicting good health | Health protection depends on affordable and reachable services, not transfers alone |
| Gendered labor and care burden | Women entered informal work after migration while retaining unpaid household and caregiving responsibilities | Contextualizes employment improvement despite continued health vulnerability | Employment gains may improve livelihood stability but do not necessarily reduce health risk |
| Poor sanitation and overcrowding | Shared toilets, unsafe water, and crowded housing were repeatedly linked to illness | Supports the role of structural living conditions in shaping health outcomes | Health vulnerability is produced by the slum environment as much as by individual resources |
| Reliance on informal networks | Neighbors, kin, NGO workers, and community actors helped women access information, jobs, and occasional support | Explains variation in access to aid and services | Social capital can help women navigate institutions but cannot replace formal protection |
| Displacement pathway differences | Women displaced by drought, food insecurity, and slow livelihood collapse described prolonged stress before migration | Supports observed variation in disease burden by displacement type | Climate-displaced women should not be treated as a single uniform group |
